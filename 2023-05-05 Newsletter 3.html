Highlights from recent discussions in Leaders of Awesomeness

Highlights from some fascinating UX leadership discussions we found in the Leaders of Awesomeness community.


What are your thoughts?

1

How should we talk about “power users”?


Mitch Malone asked:


“What do you think of the term "power user" and its framing?”


Mitch defines a power user as someone who uses advanced features of the product, or who uses certain features more intensely or to a higher degree of productivity than other users.


How have you talked about power users?

2

Should research be tracked separately from other things?


After watching the recent Talk UX Strategy discussion on Conducting Research in an Agile Environment,  Tristan Bailey asked:


“How does one determine and prioritize what needs to be researched?”


Tristan wondered if a second backlog or queue for theories or broad areas of interest is an appropriate strategy.

  • Jared Spool said that, in his experience, UX leaders use a formal research agenda — a sort of high-level roadmap guided by the product roadmap filtered through the lens of research.

How do you track your research questions?

3

Sharing the work of team members


An excellent discussion continues on Bethany Kilgore’s question:


“In what ways does your team share the work each person is doing?”

  • Vajira Nissanka said their team uses a Slack channel to report on their team’s work. Vajira added that recently the team has begun choosing one project from each sprint to present to the whole Product team every other week. 

  • Jordan Clarke agreed that keeping teams cognizant of each others’ work is often challenging and that their team has a Teams channel. Jordan’s team also uses Loom or other recordings of streams to share demos and design rationales. Jordan also shared that their organization has set aside a “13th week” learning and development period for individuals to present a deep dive on the products in their portfolios.

  • Benjamin Perry suggested the “Atomic Research” model and helpfully provided a link to the article by Daniel Pidcock explaining the use and usefulness of the concept.

Add your thoughts on keeping your team connected.
